The Transformer Architecture: From Attention to Embeddings

The transformer is the architectural foundation underlying almost every modern language model. Understanding how it works — not just that it works — makes it much easier to reason about what models can and can't do, and why embedding-based search behaves the way it does.

The key idea is self-attention. Given a sequence of tokens, self-attention allows every token to "look at" every other token and decide how much to weight each one when computing its own representation. This is done by projecting each token into three vectors — a query, a key, and a value. The attention score between two tokens is the dot product of one's query and the other's key, scaled by the square root of the dimensionality to keep gradients stable. These scores are passed through a softmax to produce weights, and then used to compute a weighted sum of the value vectors. The result is a new representation for each token that incorporates context from the whole sequence.

Multi-head attention runs this process in parallel across several independent "heads," each with its own learned projections. Different heads tend to capture different types of relationships — some heads attend to syntactic structure, others to coreference, others to semantic similarity. The outputs of all heads are concatenated and projected back to the original dimension.

A full transformer layer adds two more components after the attention sublayer: a position-wise feed-forward network (essentially two linear layers with a nonlinearity in between), and layer normalisation with residual connections around both sublayers. The residual connections are crucial — they let gradients flow directly through the network during training, which is what makes it practical to stack many layers deep.

Positional encoding is how transformers handle sequence order. Unlike RNNs, which process tokens one at a time and inherently encode position through the order of computation, transformers process all tokens simultaneously. To preserve ordering information, positional encodings are added to the token embeddings before feeding them into the first layer. The original paper used sinusoidal encodings; many modern models learn the positional embeddings directly from data.

Encoder models like BERT are trained to produce contextualised representations of input text. During pre-training, BERT masks random tokens and learns to predict them from context (masked language modelling). This forces the model to build rich representations that capture meaning from both left and right context. The resulting representations are what you use when you want to compute sentence embeddings for semantic search.

Sentence transformers are a specific family of models fine-tuned to produce good sentence-level embeddings. They typically use a mean pooling layer over the final encoder output tokens to produce a fixed-size vector for the whole input sequence. The training objective is contrastive: pairs of semantically similar sentences are pulled together in the embedding space, while dissimilar pairs are pushed apart. Models like all-MiniLM-L6-v2 and all-mpnet-base-v2 were trained this way on large datasets of sentence pairs.

The dimension of the embedding matters in practice. all-MiniLM-L6-v2 produces 384-dimensional vectors and is fast enough to use interactively on CPU. all-mpnet-base-v2 produces 768-dimensional vectors, is more accurate, but takes roughly twice as long. Larger models like e5-large produce 1024-dimensional vectors and are even more accurate, at greater cost. For most search applications, the 384d model is the right default — it's fast, the quality is good, and you can always upgrade later if benchmarking shows it's the bottleneck.

Normalisation before storing embeddings is important. If you L2-normalise your vectors before storing them, cosine similarity reduces to a simple dot product. This matters because HNSW indexes in most vector databases use dot product internally, and unnormalised vectors can produce misleading similarity scores. The sentence-transformers library handles this automatically if you pass normalize_embeddings=True.

Cross-encoders are a different kind of model used in reranking. Instead of encoding query and document separately and comparing the resulting vectors, a cross-encoder takes the query and document concatenated together and produces a single relevance score. This is much slower (you have to run the model for every query-document pair, not just once per query), but it's more accurate because the model can directly attend to both at once. The standard pattern is to use a bi-encoder for initial retrieval (fast, approximate) and a cross-encoder to rerank the top results (slow, accurate).

Understanding these architectural details pays off when you're debugging search quality. If your semantic search is missing obviously relevant documents, the first thing to check is whether the query and the relevant passages use the same vocabulary and style — embedding models can struggle with domain shift. The second thing to check is chunking: if passages are too long, the embedding averages over too much content and loses the specific signal you need.
