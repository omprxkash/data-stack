Information Retrieval: From Keyword Search to Semantic Systems

Information retrieval is the science of finding material — usually documents — that is relevant to an information need within a large collection. The field is older than the web: formal IR research dates to the 1950s, when computing was still a novelty and the challenge of organising large document collections was already pressing.

The Boolean retrieval model was the first formal approach. Users wrote structured queries with AND, OR, and NOT operators, and documents either matched or didn't. Boolean retrieval is still used in many specialised databases because it gives users precise control. The limitation is that it provides no way to rank results — every matching document is equally valid, and in large collections you can easily return thousands of documents that all technically match but vary enormously in relevance.

Term frequency–inverse document frequency, almost always abbreviated to TF-IDF, was a major step forward. The idea is to weight a term's importance in a document by how often it appears there (term frequency), while penalising terms that appear in most documents in the corpus (inverse document frequency). The word "the" appears everywhere and tells you nothing about a document's topic; the phrase "spectral decomposition" appearing three times in a document is much more informative. TF-IDF captures this intuition cleanly.

BM25, short for Best Match 25, is a refinement of TF-IDF that addresses some of its weaknesses. Raw TF-IDF grows without bound as term frequency increases, which can over-reward documents that simply repeat a term many times. BM25 applies a saturation function: additional occurrences of a term keep adding relevance, but with diminishing returns. BM25 also accounts for document length — long documents naturally contain more words, so they should be penalised slightly relative to short documents that match the query just as well. Despite being defined in the 1990s, BM25 remains the dominant baseline in information retrieval benchmarks.

Dense retrieval changed the picture significantly. Rather than matching keywords, dense retrieval encodes both the query and each document into a shared vector space using neural networks, then finds the documents whose vectors are closest to the query vector. This allows the system to find relevant documents even when they use entirely different vocabulary from the query — a query about "fixing a water leak" can match a document that talks about "plumbing repair" and "sealing pipe joints," even though none of those words appear in the query.

The dual-encoder architecture is the standard approach for dense retrieval. A query encoder and a document encoder (often the same model fine-tuned to handle both roles) independently produce embedding vectors. At query time, you embed the query and find the nearest document vectors using an approximate nearest-neighbour index. Because the documents are encoded offline in advance, the only runtime cost is one forward pass through the query encoder plus a fast index lookup.

Hybrid retrieval systems combine BM25 and dense retrieval, typically by computing both scores and then combining them with a weighted sum or by using a learned fusion model. The intuition is that they catch different types of relevant documents: BM25 is strong on exact lexical matches and technical jargon, while dense retrieval is stronger on paraphrase and conceptual similarity. Together they consistently outperform either method alone on most retrieval benchmarks.

Reranking is a separate stage that improves the precision of the top results. After an initial retrieval stage identifies candidate documents, a reranker — usually a cross-encoder model that jointly attends to both the query and the document — produces a more accurate relevance score. Cross-encoders are too slow to run over an entire corpus, but they're practical for reranking the top 20 or 50 candidates from the first-stage retrieval.

Evaluation in IR is built on test collections: a set of documents, a set of queries, and relevance judgments from human annotators. Precision measures the fraction of retrieved documents that are actually relevant. Recall measures the fraction of all relevant documents that were retrieved. Mean Average Precision (MAP) and Normalised Discounted Cumulative Gain (NDCG) give more nuanced rankings-aware metrics. Mean Reciprocal Rank (MRR) is useful when you care primarily about whether the first relevant document appears near the top.
